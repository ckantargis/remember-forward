# Universal Symbol Research for Time-Capsule Plate Design

**Purpose:** Reference document for Remember Forward plate redesign. Identifies which visual symbols are genuinely cross-cultural and cross-temporal, and which ones we falsely assume to be universal.

**Use case:** Laser-engraved SVG plates intended to be readable by a finder 200-2,000 years from now with no shared language, writing system, or cultural context.

## 1. Prohibition / "DO NOT" Symbols

### The Circle-Slash (Red Circle with Diagonal Line)

**How universal is it really?**

The circle-slash prohibition sign is formally defined in ISO 3864-1 (first published 1984, revised 2002 and 2011). The design specifies a red circle with a 45-degree diagonal line from upper-left to lower-right, overlaid on a pictogram of the prohibited action. The red area must cover at least 35% of the total sign area.

**Cross-cultural status:** The symbol is now globally distributed through ISO standards, the Vienna Convention on Road Signs and Signals, and international aviation/maritime signage. No documented cases exist of the circle-slash being fundamentally misunderstood or carrying a contradictory meaning in any culture. However, this universality is *recent* -- it is a product of 20th-century standardization, not deep cultural evolution. A person from a pre-industrial or isolated culture has no reason to intuit "prohibition" from a circle with a line through it.

**Key limitation for our use case:** The circle-slash is a *learned convention*, not an innate human response. It has no archaeological precedent. A finder 1,000 years from now in a post-collapse society would have no inherent reason to read it as "do not."

**Regional design variations (not misinterpretations):**
- Vienna Convention road signs use red-bordered circles *without* diagonal lines for vehicle restrictions
- German traffic signs use solid red discs with white horizontal bars for "no entry"
- These are variants within the same cultural framework, not true cross-cultural divergence

### Historical and Archaeological Equivalents

There are no known pre-modern equivalents of an abstract "prohibition" symbol. Before standardization, prohibition was communicated through:

- **Physical barriers** (walls, thorns, moats) -- the oldest "do not enter" signals
- **Threatening imagery** (gargoyles, guardian figures, fierce deity statues at temple entrances)
- **Territorial markings** (animal urine, cairns marking boundaries)
- **Narrative scenes** showing consequences of transgression (common in religious art worldwide)

### ISO 7010 Prohibition Sign Standard

ISO 7010:2019 defines registered safety signs including prohibition signs. The standard is designed for cross-border comprehension. However, ISO 7010 itself acknowledges cultural limits: for symbols containing a first-aid cross, the standard notes it "may be replaced with another element appropriate to cultural requirements" (e.g., a crescent in Muslim-majority countries). This is an explicit admission that even within the ISO system, some symbols require cultural adaptation.

### More Primitive Equivalents a Pre-Literate Finder Might Intuit

The WIPP (Waste Isolation Pilot Plant) nuclear waste marker research (Sandia National Laboratories, 1993) is the most directly relevant study. Their conclusion: **show consequences, not abstract symbols.**

The WIPP team proposed:
- **Hostile architecture**: landscapes of thorns, spike fields, forbidding blocks -- physical forms that evoke danger and repulsion without any learned symbolism
- **Pictographic sequences**: stick figures approaching a site, then becoming sick and dying -- a visual narrative that requires no cultural knowledge beyond recognizing human figures and distress
- **Distressed facial expressions**: based on the premise that the human fear/pain response is biologically universal (supported by Paul Ekman's cross-cultural facial expression research)

**Recommendation for plates:** Rather than a circle-slash, show a pictographic sequence of the prohibited action leading to a negative consequence (sick/dead figure). This is the WIPP approach scaled down.

## 2. Confirmation / "DO THIS / SAFE" Symbols

### The Checkmark -- NOT Cross-Culturally Universal

This is one of the most important findings for our project. **The checkmark is actively misleading in multiple major cultural contexts.**

**Documented reversals:**
- **Japan:** The O mark (maru, circle) means "correct." The check mark or X mark (batsu) means "wrong/incorrect." A checkmark on our plates would be read as "wrong" or "do not" by a Japanese cultural inheritor.
- **South Korea:** Same convention -- circle (gongpyo) = correct, X = wrong.
- **Sweden and Norway:** The checkmark means "wrong" in schools. "R" (from *ratt*/*riktig*, meaning "correct") marks right answers.
- **Finland:** The checkmark means *vaarin* ("wrong"). Correct answers use a dot-based symbol.

**The circle (O) as "correct/yes":** While the circle means "correct" in East Asia, it means "zero/nothing" in many Western contexts. Neither the checkmark nor the circle is safe as a universal affirmative.

### What Research Says Is More Universally "Yes/Correct/Safe"

No single abstract mark reliably means "yes" across all cultures. The most robust approaches are:

1. **Show the positive outcome**: A healthy, upright human figure with visible signs of wellbeing (e.g., standing tall, arms relaxed or raised). This leverages biological recognition of healthy body posture.
2. **Pairing with negative**: Show a before/after or side-by-side comparison where the "correct" path leads to a healthy figure and the "incorrect" path leads to a sick/dead figure. Contrast is more universally readable than any single affirmative mark.
3. **The open hand / palm-forward gesture**: Some researchers consider the open palm a near-universal "peace/no threat" gesture across primates, but this is debated and culturally loaded in some contexts.

### Thumbs Up -- NOT Universal, Actively Offensive in Some Cultures

- **Western countries:** Approval, "good," "OK"
- **Afghanistan, Iran, parts of Iraq:** Equivalent to showing the middle finger in the West -- highly offensive
- **Greece, parts of Italy:** Can be dismissive or rude
- **Middle East broadly:** Often offensive or disrespectful

**Verdict:** Thumbs up must be avoided entirely on plates intended for cross-cultural interpretation.

## 3. Warning / Danger Symbols

### Triangle with Exclamation Mark

**History:** The triangle-exclamation warning symbol dates to the 1970s as a standardized form. Triangular warning shapes on road signs emerged in the 1960s. It is a product of modern industrial standardization with no pre-modern precedent.

**Universality:** Widely recognized in industrialized nations through road signage. Used in Europe and most of the world (but notably NOT standard in North America or Australia for road signs, where diamond-shaped warning signs are used instead). This regional split even within modern industrialized nations demonstrates that it is not innately understood.

**For our use case:** The triangle-exclamation is a learned convention. It has no archaeological depth and no biological basis. A post-collapse finder would not intuit "warning" from this shape.

### Skull and Crossbones

**History and cross-cultural reception:**
- **Late Medieval Europe:** Originated as a memento mori symbol on tombstones, representing death and mortality
- **Ancient Egypt:** Carved or painted skulls placed on tombs of the deceased
- **Medieval plague:** Marked on doors of infected houses and burial sites as a warning to stay away
- **Spanish cemeteries:** Actual skulls and bones marked entrances to *campo santo*
- **Piracy (17th-18th century):** Adopted as the Jolly Roger flag
- **Alchemy:** Used to represent sulfur and transmutation processes
- **Hinduism:** Lord Shiva and Goddess Kali wear garlands of skulls in their severe forms
- **Tibetan/Nepalese Buddhism:** Deities like Kurukulla wear skull necklaces
- **Aztec/Pre-Columbian:** The skull honored and treated with cultural importance (not necessarily as "danger")

**Critical problem for our use case:** The skull has extremely varied meanings across cultures:
- Death/danger (Western modern)
- Mortality/memento mori (Medieval European)
- Sacred power/divine energy (Hindu, Tibetan Buddhist)
- Ancestor veneration/honor (Aztec, Mexican)
- Rebellion/identity (pirate, punk subculture)

Carl Sagan suggested the skull and crossbones for the WIPP nuclear markers, but the Sandia team ultimately did not rely on it alone because of these cultural ambiguities. A skull might be read as "sacred site" or "place of honor" rather than "danger."

**Verdict:** The skull is suggestive of death across most cultures but not reliably interpreted as "danger/stay away." Use it only in context (e.g., as part of a narrative sequence showing a person dying), never as an isolated symbol.

### Fire Symbol

Fire symbolism appears in virtually every culture and every era. It is one of the few genuinely universal human experiences -- every culture has direct experience with fire. However, fire's symbolic meanings are deeply dual:

- **Positive:** Warmth, protection, purification, divine presence, creation, rebirth
- **Negative:** Destruction, punishment, danger, pain

**Visual representation:** A stylized flame shape (pointed at top, wider at base) is widely recognizable. The Mayan glyph K'ak represents fire as a flame shape. The Nordic Futhark *ken* rune represents fire. Egyptian hieroglyphs include fire determinatives.

**For our use case:** A flame pictogram is highly likely to be recognized as "fire" across cultures and time periods. The challenge is conveying whether fire is the *danger* or the *tool*. Context (narrative sequence) is essential.

### Wavy Lines for Water

The use of undulating or zigzag lines to represent water has deep archaeological roots:

- **Egyptian hieroglyphics:** The water determinative (three wavy horizontal lines, Unicode U+13196) is one of the oldest standardized water symbols. The number of ripples varies but the ends always slant downward.
- **Chinese:** The character "chuan" (river radical) uses three wavy vertical lines
- **Mesopotamian cuneiform:** Water represented by wavy line patterns
- **Pre-Columbian art:** Wavy/zigzag lines associated with water across Mesoamerican traditions

**Cross-cultural robustness:** Wavy horizontal lines are among the most reliably cross-cultural water representations. Art historians and archaeologists recognize this as a symbol of considerable antiquity. The visual metaphor is direct -- wavy lines mimic the appearance of water's surface -- making it one of the rare cases where the symbol is *iconic* (looks like the thing it represents) rather than *arbitrary* (assigned meaning by convention).

**Verdict:** Wavy lines for water are among the safest cross-cultural symbols available. Highly recommended for plates.

## 4. Sequence / Direction Symbols

### Arrows as Direction Indicators

**Archaeological depth:**
- Humans have been making bows and arrows for at least 64,000 years (evidence from Sibudu Cave, South Africa)
- The oldest known arrowheads date to approximately 74,000 years ago in Ethiopia
- Cave art at Cova dels Cavalls (eastern Spain, ~7,000 years ago) shows archers hunting deer with clearly directional arrow imagery
- European Paleolithic art shows animals pierced with arrows

**As direction indicators:** The arrow is one of the oldest technologies known to humanity. Its shape -- pointed end indicating direction of travel, fletched end indicating origin -- is a direct physical metaphor. Unlike most symbols, the arrow's meaning derives from a universally known physical object.

**Limitations:**
- The *drawn* arrow as a directional indicator (as opposed to a depiction of an actual projectile) is a more modern convention
- In cave art, arrows appear as weapons, not as abstract direction markers
- The abstract use of arrows for "go this way" is documented primarily from the modern era

**For our use case:** Arrows are a strong choice because the underlying object (the projectile) is among the most widespread human technologies. Even if the abstract convention is lost, the physical metaphor of "pointy end = direction of movement" is grounded in physics that does not change. However, reinforce arrows with sequential pictographic context rather than relying on a bare arrow symbol.

### Numbered Sequences: Arabic Numerals vs. Tally Marks vs. Dots

**Arabic (Hindu-Arabic) numerals:**
- Invented between the 1st and 4th centuries by Indian mathematicians
- Adopted by Arabic mathematicians by the 9th century
- Now globally dominant but NOT visually intuitive. The shapes 1, 2, 3... are arbitrary -- there is nothing about the shape "7" that communicates "seven of something"
- Different numeral glyphs are used in different regions (Eastern Arabic numerals in the Middle East look different from Western Arabic numerals)

**Tally marks:**
- The oldest known tally sticks date to 35,000-25,000 years ago (notched bones from the European Aurignacian/Gravettian and Africa's Late Stone Age)
- Fundamentally intuitive: one mark = one unit. No learning required.
- Different cultures group tallies differently (Western: groups of 5 with diagonal strike-through; Chinese/Japanese: using the character for "correct" with 5 strokes; South American: groups of varied sizes)
- Limitation: unwieldy for numbers above ~10-15

**Dot systems:**
- Mayan numerals use dots (1 each) and horizontal bars (5 each) -- independently invented
- Dots are among von Petzinger's 32 recurring Ice Age geometric signs
- A single dot is arguably the most primitive possible counting unit

**Recommendation for plates:** Use dots (filled circles) for counting sequences 1-5. For steps beyond 5, use dot groups. Avoid Arabic numerals entirely -- they are a learned convention that may not survive. Tally marks (simple vertical strokes) are the second-best option and can handle larger numbers. Dots and tallies together form a redundant, robust counting system.

## 5. The ISOTYPE System (Otto Neurath, 1920s-40s)

### What It Was

ISOTYPE (International System of Typographic Picture Education) was developed at the Social and Economic Museum of Vienna between 1925 and 1934, with Otto Neurath as founding director. Originally called the "Vienna Method of Pictorial Statistics," it was a system of standardized, abstracted pictorial symbols designed to represent social-scientific data using specific rules for combination and repetition.

Neurath's slogan: **"Words divide, pictures unite."**

### Core Design Principles

1. **Quantity by repetition, not size:** Greater quantities are shown by repeating identical pictograms, never by enlarging them. Neurath believed size variation prevented accurate comparison.
2. **No perspective:** Pictograms almost never depict things in perspective, to preserve clarity.
3. **Minimal necessary detail:** "The symbol may not denote more than is necessary to the statement of facts."
4. **Layered reading:** "At the first look you see the most important points, at the second the less important points, at the third the details, at the fourth nothing more."
5. **Concrete, not abstract:** Pictures can only show concrete, tangible objects. They cannot express abstract entities. This is a *feature*, not a bug -- it forces clarity.

### What It Got Right

- Pictograms based on recognizable physical objects (people, buildings, ships, animals) communicate across language barriers
- Systematic rules for composition prevent ambiguity
- The principle of showing concrete things rather than abstractions is directly applicable to survival knowledge plates
- The human figure as a basic unit of communication proved highly effective
- Repetition as a counting mechanism is more intuitive than size scaling

### Documented Limitations

1. **Some objects resist typification:** Neurath's team found it "seems impossible to design a recognizable pictogram for potato." Many everyday objects simply do not have distinctive silhouettes.
2. **Testing was not systematic:** "The scientific dimension of Isotype work was established by the empirical status of its source sciences," but comprehension testing was "hardly strict or systematic."
3. **Cultural assumptions:** The pictograms were designed within a European cultural framework. Objects common in European daily life were prioritized.
4. **The "transformer" bottleneck:** Converting expert knowledge into pictographic form required a skilled intermediary (the "transformer") who had to balance expert detail with user comprehension. This role is inherently subjective.

### Principles Still Considered Valid

- Concrete depiction over abstraction
- Consistent sizing and style across a symbol set
- Layered information density (most important visible first)
- The human figure as a universal anchor point
- Repetition for quantity
- Avoidance of perspective distortion

## 6. ISO 7010 and Humanitarian/UN Pictogram Systems

### ISO 7010 and ISO 7001

**ISO 7010:2019** defines registered safety signs (prohibition, warning, mandatory action, safe condition, fire equipment). **ISO 7001** defines public information symbols (wayfinding, facilities, services).

**Testing:** ISO 7001 symbols are tested across multiple countries and cultures. The design and testing process is governed by ISO 22727:2007. Documented comprehension rates for specific symbols:
- Tickets pictogram: 97% correct interpretation (An & Chan, 2017, tested in Chinese transit hubs)
- Healthcare center (white cross on dark background): 75% correct in USA testing
- Books/magazines pictogram: best variant achieved 72% correct (Brugger, 1983)

**Comprehension thresholds:**
- ISO recommends **67% comprehension** for a pictogram to be considered successful
- ANSI (American National Standards Institute) recommends **85% comprehension**

**Critical finding:** Even within the ISO system, a 67% threshold means one-third of people may misunderstand any given symbol. For a survival plate that must work across millennia, this is unacceptable.

### UN/OCHA Humanitarian Icons

The United Nations Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA) created a set of 500+ freely available humanitarian icons (first set of 250 in 2012, expanded to 295+ in 2018). These are designed for rapid communication during emergencies across language barriers.

**Design challenges encountered:**
- "There are no naturally universal symbols" -- this is OCHA's own assessment
- Widely distributed symbols tend to be based on widely distributed *artifacts* (like the arrow) or widely distributed *forms of representation* (like the sun)
- Person icons originally labeled "Man" and "Woman" had to be renamed "Person 1" and "Person 2" to avoid culturally specific gender stereotyping
- Some icons were interpreted with opposite emotional valence across cultures: one pictogram read as "friends" by Americans was read as "liar" or "deceitful" by Japanese respondents

### Hablamos Juntos Healthcare Symbol System

Developed in the United States, this is one of the most rigorously tested universal healthcare symbol sets. A study of Taiwanese adoption found:
- 11 symbols were "highly comprehensible and effective"
- 16 symbols were "incomprehensible or confusing and had to be redesigned"

This is a ~40% failure rate for symbols that were *designed* to be universal and *tested* before deployment. It underscores that cross-cultural symbol comprehension cannot be assumed.

## 7. Symbols to AVOID

### Confirmed Cross-Cultural Failures

| Symbol | Assumed Meaning | Actual Problem |
|--------|----------------|----------------|
| **Checkmark** | "Correct/yes" | Means "wrong" in Japan, Korea, Sweden, Norway, Finland |
| **Thumbs up** | "Good/approval" | Obscene gesture in Iran, Afghanistan, parts of Iraq, Greece |
| **Red color** | "Danger/stop" | Means "luck/prosperity" in China; "mourning" in South Africa |
| **White color** | "Purity/clean" | Means "mourning/death" in China, Japan, Korea, India |
| **Green color** | "Safety/go" | Religious connotations in Islamic cultures; "infidelity" in China |
| **Skull & crossbones** (isolated) | "Danger/poison" | "Sacred power" (Hindu/Buddhist); "honor/ancestors" (Aztec/Mexican) |
| **Triangle-exclamation** | "Warning" | No meaning outside industrial societies; diamond-shaped in N. America/Australia |
| **First-aid cross (+)** | "Medical help" | Religiously loaded (Christian cross); ISO 7010 itself allows crescent substitute |
| **Circle (O)** | "Nothing/zero" (West) | "Correct/yes" (Japan/Korea) |
| **X mark** | "Wrong/no" (West) | "Correct" in some East Asian grading; "marks the spot" (treasure maps) |
| **Raised hand** | "Stop" | "Blessing" in some religious contexts; offensive quantity gestures vary |

### Symbols Whose Meanings Reverse by Culture

The most dangerous category is symbols that don't merely fail to communicate but actively communicate the *opposite* of the intended meaning. The checkmark is the canonical example: using it to mean "do this" on a plate could be read as "do NOT do this" by a Japanese-culture inheritor.

### Color as a Symbol

Color is unreliable for cross-temporal communication regardless of cultural issues, because:
1. Laser-engraved metal plates have no color -- they are monochromatic
2. Color meanings vary dramatically across cultures (documented above)
3. Color fades, corrodes, and changes over centuries

Our plates are already monochromatic, which is correct. Do not rely on color distinctions in any future variants.

## 8. Most Primitive/Robust Symbol Set

### Evidence from Cave Art (40,000+ Years)

Genevieve von Petzinger's research documented exactly **32 geometric sign types** used repeatedly across European Ice Age cave art spanning 30,000 years (40,000 to 10,000 years ago). Many of these same signs appear in cave art in Africa, Asia, Australia, and the Americas, suggesting possible universal human symbolic tendencies.

**The 32 signs include:**
- Dots (among the most common and widespread)
- Lines (vertical, horizontal, grouped)
- Circles and semicircles
- Spirals
- Negative hands (handprint outlines -- among the oldest, 22,000-40,000 years ago)
- Zigzag/wavy lines
- Crosshatch patterns
- Penniforms (feather/plume shapes)
- Tectiforms (roof shapes -- these were the most *localized*, appearing at only 9 sites)
- Asterisks/star shapes
- Triangles
- Quadrangles/rectangles

**Key finding:** The small, stable set of only 32 signs across 30,000 years and an entire continent means these were meaningful and deliberately transmitted, not random. They represent the deepest known layer of human symbolic communication.

### Universally Recurring Symbols Across Uncontacted Civilizations

Cross-cultural anthropological research identifies these as recurring across independently developed symbol systems:

1. **The human figure** (stick figure / outline form) -- appears in cave art on every inhabited continent, recognized by children worldwide before formal education
2. **The sun** (circle with radiating lines) -- independently developed in Egyptian, Chinese, Aztec, Aboriginal Australian, and Nordic symbol systems
3. **Water** (wavy/zigzag horizontal lines) -- Egyptian hieroglyphs, Chinese characters, Mesopotamian cuneiform, Mesoamerican art
4. **Hand** (outline or negative impression) -- among the oldest known symbols, appearing from 40,000 years ago on every continent
5. **Dots/tallies** (counting marks) -- 35,000+ year archaeological record across Africa and Europe
6. **The arrow/pointed shape** -- derived from the physical projectile (64,000+ years of arrow technology)
7. **Animal figures** -- universally depicted in representational art
8. **Flame/fire shape** -- independently developed pictographic representation across cultures

### What Children with No Education Reliably Interpret

Developmental psychology research suggests children reliably recognize:
- **Human figures** (stick figures) from approximately age 3-4
- **Facial expressions** (happy, sad, afraid, angry) from infancy -- these are the most biologically robust symbols available
- **Animals** (simplified/schematic forms of familiar species)
- **Size relationships** (bigger = more/stronger)
- **Cause-and-effect sequences** when shown in pictographic narrative form
- **Physical damage** (broken objects, fallen figures)

### The WIPP Nuclear Marker Conclusions

The most directly relevant research to our project. The Sandia team's conclusions after extensive expert consultation:

1. **Pictorial narratives are supreme**: "Humans inherently like and understand pictorial narratives." Sequential imagery showing before/during/after is the most robust form of cross-temporal communication.
2. **Stick figures work**: "Everybody recognizes [the stick figure] as the human shape, by nature." Traceable to prehistoric cave art.
3. **Top-to-bottom reading is natural**: "Everyone, seemingly by nature, reads from top to bottom."
4. **Learned symbols fail**: "Icons which require teaching to understand their meaning may lack staying power." The biohazard symbol, radiation trefoil, and similar abstract signs were rejected.
5. **Show consequences, not prohibitions**: Rather than saying "do not," show what happens if you do. A figure approaching danger, then becoming sick/dying, communicates across all cultural contexts.
6. **Defense in depth**: Use multiple redundant communication layers. Never rely on a single symbol.
7. **Hostile architecture communicates**: Physical form (spikes, jagged shapes, uncomfortable angles) can communicate "unwelcome/danger" without any symbolic convention.

## 9. Recommendations for Our Use Cases

For each use case, the recommendation prioritizes:
- **Iconic** symbols (look like what they represent) over **arbitrary** symbols (meaning assigned by convention)
- **Pictographic narrative** (sequential scenes) over isolated symbols
- **Biological universals** (facial expressions, body posture, cause-and-effect) over cultural conventions
- **Redundancy** (multiple cues reinforcing the same message)

---

### DIRTY (Unsafe/Contaminated) Water vs. CLEAN (Safe) Water

**Recommended approach:**
- Water in both cases: **wavy horizontal lines** (one of the most archaeologically robust water symbols, independently developed across Egyptian, Chinese, Mesopotamian, and Mesoamerican writing systems)
- **DIRTY water:** Wavy lines with small dots/particles suspended in them (visual metaphor for contamination -- particulate matter is visible in dirty water universally). Adjacent pictographic panel: stick figure drinking from this source, then becoming sick (hunched posture, wavy lines emanating from stomach suggesting nausea)
- **CLEAN water:** Wavy lines without particles, clean/clear. Adjacent pictographic panel: stick figure drinking, then standing upright and healthy
- **Side-by-side comparison** is strongest: the contrast between the two outcomes reinforces the message without requiring any single symbol to carry the full semantic load

**Avoid:** Checkmarks/X marks, color coding (plates are monochromatic), skull and crossbones in isolation, the circle-slash prohibition sign

---

### DO THIS (Correct Procedure) vs. DO NOT DO THIS

**Recommended approach:**
- **DO THIS:** Show the action being performed by a stick figure, followed by a panel showing the positive outcome (healthy/upright figure, successful result). Use a **clear directional flow** (left-to-right or top-to-bottom sequence with arrow connectors).
- **DO NOT DO THIS:** Show the action being performed, followed by a panel showing the negative outcome (figure hunched/fallen/sick/injured). The WIPP research confirms this "show consequences" approach is the most cross-temporally robust method.
- **Pairing:** Always show both sequences (correct and incorrect) adjacent to each other. The contrast between good and bad outcomes is more powerful than any isolated "yes" or "no" symbol.
- **Structural cue:** Consider using a visual divider or different framing (e.g., the incorrect sequence could have jagged/spiked border elements -- hostile architecture at micro scale -- while the correct sequence has smooth borders)

**Avoid:** Checkmarks, X marks, circle-slash, thumbs up/down, any isolated abstract affirmative/negative symbols

---

### DANGER / WARNING

**Recommended approach:**
- **Primary:** Pictographic narrative showing a stick figure encountering the hazard and suffering consequences (injury, sickness, death -- shown as a prone/fallen figure)
- **Secondary reinforcement:** Jagged/spiked border elements around danger zones (the WIPP "hostile architecture" principle scaled to graphic design -- angular, uncomfortable shapes that evoke threat at a visceral level)
- **Facial expression:** If figure detail permits at engraving scale, distressed facial expressions (mouth open, brow furrowed) leverage biologically innate recognition of fear/pain expressions
- **Skull** can be used as part of a death-consequence narrative (figure becomes skeleton) but never as an isolated danger symbol

**Avoid:** Triangle-exclamation (no pre-modern precedent, not used in N. America/Australia), abstract warning symbols, color-dependent warnings

---

### SEQUENCE STEPS (1, 2, 3...)

**Recommended approach:**
- **Dots** (filled circles): The most primitive counting symbol, part of von Petzinger's 32 recurring Ice Age signs, independently developed in Mayan numerals
  - Step 1: one dot
  - Step 2: two dots
  - Step 3: three dots
  - Steps 4-5: four/five dots
- For sequences longer than 5, use **dot groups with spatial separation**
- **Reinforce with spatial flow:** Arrange panels left-to-right or top-to-bottom (top-to-bottom is more universally natural per WIPP research). Connect with **arrow shapes** pointing in the direction of sequence progression.
- **Each step is a panel:** Self-contained pictographic scene showing the action for that step, with the dot-count in a consistent position (e.g., upper-left corner of each panel)

**Avoid:** Arabic numerals (arbitrary learned convention, glyphs vary by region), Roman numerals (culturally specific), lettering of any kind

---

### TIME PASSING (Wait, Duration)

**Recommended approach:**
This is one of the hardest concepts to communicate cross-temporally because time is abstract. Options:

- **Sun arc sequence:** Show the sun in multiple positions across a sky-line (rising, overhead, setting) to represent the passage of a day. The sun's path is universally observed. Multiple sun-arcs = multiple days.
- **Moon phase sequence:** Show moon phases (crescent to full to crescent) for longer durations. Moon phases are universally observed across all cultures.
- **Hourglass / dripping water:** Less universally recognizable as "time" -- the hourglass is a European invention, and "time = dripping" requires cultural knowledge.
- **Plant growth sequence:** A seed becoming a sprout becoming a plant. This communicates "time passes and growth occurs" through a universally observed natural process.

**Best for short durations (hours):** Sun position changes
**Best for medium durations (days):** Repeated sun arcs with a count
**Best for long durations (seasons/years):** Plant growth or moon phase cycles

**Avoid:** Clock faces (culturally specific technology), hourglass (European), abstract time notation

---

### HEAT / FIRE

**Recommended approach:**
- **Flame pictogram:** Pointed at top, wider at base, with optional interior wavy lines suggesting movement. This shape is independently developed across Egyptian, Mayan (K'ak glyph), Norse (ken rune), and many other symbol systems. It is an iconic representation -- it looks like a flame.
- **Wavy rising lines above a surface:** Represent heat radiation/shimmer. These are visible to anyone who has seen heat distortion above a hot surface.
- **Combine both:** Flame shape below, wavy heat-rise lines above
- **Context matters:** To distinguish "fire is the tool" from "fire is the danger," use the pictographic narrative approach:
  - Fire as tool: figure uses fire, positive outcome follows
  - Fire as danger: figure contacts fire, injury follows (figure recoils, hand shown damaged)

### PERSON (Human Figure)

**Recommended approach:**
- **Stick figure:** "Everybody recognizes it as the human shape, by nature" (WIPP research). Traceable to cave art 30,000+ years ago. One of the most universally recognized symbols in human history.
- **Design principles from ISOTYPE:** Consistent size, no perspective distortion, minimal detail sufficient for recognition
- **Gender neutrality:** Use the same basic figure for all people unless biological sex is specifically relevant (e.g., pregnancy/childbirth plates). The OCHA humanitarian icons team found gendered figure designs caused cross-cultural interpretation problems.
- **Show state through posture:**
  - Healthy: upright, limbs in natural position
  - Sick: hunched, hand on stomach or head
  - Dead/dying: prone (horizontal), or skeletal
  - Active: limbs in motion-suggesting positions

**1964 Tokyo Olympics precedent:** The first international stick-figure pictogram set, designed by Masaru Katsumi and Yoshiro Yamashita, formed the basis of all subsequent Olympic and international wayfinding pictograms. Their design worked across the dozens of languages present at the Games.

---

### SICK / ILL vs. HEALTHY

**Recommended approach:**
- **SICK:** Stick figure in hunched posture, hand on stomach or head. Wavy lines emanating from the figure (suggesting fever/nausea -- wavy emanation lines are used across cultures in art to suggest something wrong/radiating from a body). Figure could be shown vomiting (drops from mouth area) for clarity about gastrointestinal illness.
- **HEALTHY:** Stick figure standing fully upright, limbs in relaxed natural position, no emanation lines
- **Side-by-side contrast:** Place sick and healthy figures adjacent so the comparison reinforces interpretation
- **Narrative version:** Show a sequence -- figure is sick (hunched), performs the remedy action, becomes healthy (upright). This is more powerful than isolated symbols because it communicates both the problem and the solution.
- **Facial expressions** if engraving detail permits: downturned mouth for sick, neutral or upturned for healthy. These are among the most biologically robust signals humans produce (Ekman's research on universal facial expressions of emotion).

**Avoid:** Red cross / medical symbols (culturally and religiously specific), abstract health indicators, color coding

## Summary: Core Design Principles for Cross-Temporal Symbol Communication

Drawing from all the above research, the following principles should govern plate symbol design:

1. **Narrative over notation.** Show sequential pictographic stories (cause leads to effect) rather than isolated symbols. This is the single most robust finding across WIPP research, ISOTYPE principles, and cross-cultural comprehension studies.

2. **Iconic over arbitrary.** Use symbols that visually resemble what they represent (wavy lines = water, flame shape = fire, stick figure = person). Avoid symbols whose meaning is purely conventional (checkmark, circle-slash, triangle-exclamation).

3. **Consequence over prohibition.** Never say "don't" with an abstract symbol. Show what happens if you do the wrong thing (sick/dead figure). Show what happens if you do the right thing (healthy figure).

4. **Contrast and pairing.** Always show correct and incorrect, safe and unsafe, side by side. The comparison communicates more reliably than either image alone.

5. **Biological universals as anchors.** The stick figure, facial expressions, body posture (upright = healthy, prone = dead), and cause-and-effect sequences are grounded in human biology, not cultural convention.

6. **Dots for counting.** The filled circle/dot is the most archaeologically deep counting symbol (35,000+ years). Use it for sequences.

7. **Arrows for direction.** The pointed-shape-implies-direction metaphor is grounded in 64,000+ years of projectile technology. Use arrows to guide reading order.

8. **Redundancy.** Never rely on a single symbol to carry a critical meaning. Layer multiple cues: pictographic narrative + spatial flow + border treatment + body posture + consequence depiction.

9. **Top-to-bottom flow preferred.** WIPP research found this reading direction to be the most universally natural.

10. **Avoid all culturally specific conventions.** When in doubt about whether a symbol is universal, it probably is not. Default to pictographic narrative showing concrete physical actions and their observable consequences.
